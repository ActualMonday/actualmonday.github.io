Science

What is Reverberation Mapping?

Reverberation mapping is a method astronomers use to study scale/structure of the nuclei of active galaxies (AGN), by utilizing a long monitoring time in place of what we lack in spatial resolution. The method relies on the characteristic variability of a quasar’s central emission being re-emitted (“reverberated”) by more distant material, delayed (or “lagged”) by the light-crossing time. The natural temperature gradient of the system causes the peak emission of hotter, more central regions, to occur at shorter wavelengths, while regions at larger radii have peak emission at longer wavelengths. By observing the light of an AGN's accretion disk in blue and red wavelengths over a long duration of time, one can measure the delay in their variability, and by proxy know the distance between an AGN's blue and red emitting regions.

Investigating Accretion Disk Size Amoungst a Large Demographic (Bokeh Link Soon)

Most of my work lies in studying accretion disk size/structure amoungst a broad demographic of quasars. These optical-continuum reverbation mapping surveys have only been made possible in the last couple decades, and our current results have shown dramatic scatter (though mostly 3x larger) compared to the predictions of astronomer's well established standard slim disk model Shakura & Sunyaev 1973 . I have explored a previous sample of 95 quasars that are diverse and well characterized in quasar properties (luminosity, mass, eddington ratio, spectra, etc.) for correlations between this disk size model offset and these various properties to better understand our missing physics within these AGN enviornments. This was the focus of my first paper Sharp et al. 2023 which I ran exauhstively thorough population studies and logical regression statistics on these quasars. The most significant result is an anti-correlation between disk lag ratio and both luminosity and black hole mass, favoring a more contemporary physical model for disk lags called the Corona-heated Accretion-disk Reprocessing (CHAR; Sun et al. 2020) model.

Running My Own Survey Probing Accretion Disk Structure

My findings in my first paper were based purely off of accretion disk scale, as opposed to structure. Most continuum reverberation mapping studies which do probe emission structure (distance between multiple emitting regions as opposed to just two) are limited in demographic to only local, low-luminosity galaxies. I led the observation of 850 hrs of telescope time through Las Cumbres Observatory (LCO) imaging 30 quasars in griz photometry bands on a 12 hour cadence over the course of 100 days each. These quasars span 3 orders of magnitude in luminosity and mass, achieving the highest time resolution of a study of this kind, as well as highest redshift (look-back time). The telescope time was awarded through an LCO Key Project proposal, as well as 2 additionally awarded project proposals which I PI'd. This data has been used to produce the emission structure of these quasars through various image analysis and multiple lag detection (signal processing) methods. This paper (near completion) aims to combine two of my planned projects with goals in studying accretion structure within a holistic demographic, and the extrema of the quasar population characterized by dramatic luminous variability (hypervariable/changing look).

Undergrad Research

In undergrad I analyzed the stellar kinematics of a lenticular galaxy, NGC 4203, as a function of spatial location along its major axis with long slit spectroscopy of the Ca II absorption lines obtained with Keck/LRIS. We did so by utilizing penalized pixel fitting method (pPXF) to compare our galaxy spectra to a stellar library of template spectra. These template spectra form a best fit model to our galaxy spectra, made from a linear combination of multiple spectra of similar spectral classification and at rest compared to our observed galaxy. These fit ultimately enables the measurement of the velocity, velocity dispersion, and higher order kinematic moments, h3 and h4. The uncertainties associated with these kinematic results were obtained from Monte Carlo simulations. I wrote a thorough review on this analysis method for as a class term paper, in the chance anyone interested or needs help working with pPXF email me as this work isn't published, basically to avoid scraping from chatPGT.

My first research experience I gained at the Munnerlyn Astronomical Instrumentation Lab at Texas A&M. There I worked with charge coupled device (CCD) detectors, and learned there basics, and worked on a comprehensive budget/specification sheet for CCDs on the market. I was also involved in a larger group project called the Traveling Spectrophotometric Calibration (TCal) system which will be used to characterize the through-put as a function of wavelength of imaging systems of many observatories.
